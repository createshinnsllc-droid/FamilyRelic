# Video 1 — Teleprompter Script
## "What Happens When You Die?"
### Family Relic | Tydro Elite
*Target runtime: 10-12 minutes | Pace: slow, deliberate, let pauses breathe*

---

## OPEN — THE HOOK
*(Look directly into the camera. No intro. No music. Just you.)*

What happens when you die?

Not to your body.

Not to your soul — that's a different conversation.

I mean — what happens to everything you *knew?*

Everything you *felt?*

Everything you *meant to say* — but never found the right moment?

What happens to the advice you would have given your kids at 30... that you figured out at 47?

What happens to the story of who you actually *were* — underneath everything life made you perform?

...

Most of the time?

It disappears.

---

## THE PRACTICAL PAIN
*(Shift tone slightly — grounded, real)*

And I'm not just talking philosophically.

I'm talking practically.

People die every day — and their families find out about bills from collection notices.

Not a letter. Not a call from a lawyer.

A collection notice.

Because nobody knew the account existed.

People die — and their phone is locked. Their laptop is locked.

Hardware full of photos, videos, voice notes, memories — locked behind a password nobody knows.

Assets. Accounts. Wishes.

Gone. Not because they didn't exist —

but because there was no system to pass them on.

And then there's the deeper loss.

The things that *weren't* practical.

The father who never told his kids how much they drove him through the hard times.

The mother who never explained why she made the choices she made.

The grandparent whose entire philosophy of life — hard-earned, real, irreplaceable —

died in a nursing home bed without ever being recorded.

We get a tombstone.

We get two sticks crossed to make a cross.

We get a dog tag.

That's it.

That's all we leave.

---

## THE HISTORICAL FRAME
*(Lean in slightly — this is the big idea)*

But here's what I want you to understand.

This is not a new problem.

Human beings have been fighting this same battle since the beginning.

The Egyptians carved their stories into pyramid walls.

Not because they were artists.

Because they were terrified of being forgotten.

People carve names into trees.

They build monuments.

They write books.

They record music.

Every single act of human creativity — at its root —

is a biological being trying to outlast their biology.

That carving on the tree?

That's a person saying: *I was here. I mattered. Remember me.*

A frivolous attempt — maybe.

But each generation gets one step closer.

And right now — *right now* — we have tools that no previous civilization had access to.

We have AI.

We have local storage.

We have the ability to preserve not just *what* someone said —

but *how* they thought.

*How* they reasoned.

*How* they would respond to a question you haven't asked yet.

That's the leap.

---

## THE BALTIMORE STORY
*(Personal — slow down here)*

I want to tell you something someone told me.

An old man I knew from Baltimore.

He'd been through things most people don't survive.

Spent over 40 years of his life in prison.

Seen violence. Caused violence. Lived through all of it.

And one day he told me something I've never forgotten.

He said:

*"As a Black man — the first 30 to 35 years, we fight external forces.*
*Other people. Gangs. The pursuit of money. The pursuit of love.*
*All the violence that entails.*
*But if you get through that —*
*the next 35 years is all about internal battles.*
*The fight against disease.*
*Control of your body. Your mind. Your faculties.*
*That becomes the next struggle."*

I never heard it put like that before.

And I never forgot it.

But here's what hit me later:

That man — with 40 years of hard-earned wisdom —

almost took all of it to the grave.

He told *me.* In a conversation.

What about everyone he *didn't* tell?

What about his kids?

His grandkids?

What about the version of that wisdom that could have saved somebody's life —

that just... evaporated?

*That's* the problem I'm trying to solve.

---

## THE SOLUTION — FAMILY RELIC
*(Settle in — this is the vision)*

I'm building something called Family Relic.

Also known as the Digital Will.

And before I tell you what it is —

let me tell you what it's *not.*

It's not a company trying to own your data.

It's not an app that locks your memories behind a subscription.

It's not AI pretending to be your loved one in a way that feels cheap or disrespectful.

What it *is* —

is a framework.

Open source. Free. Built by a community, for a community.

A structured way for a person — *while they're alive* —

to deliberately preserve who they are.

Their voice.

Their values.

Their reasoning.

Their stories.

Their advice for moments that haven't happened yet.

Phase one is simple.

Photos. Videos. Voice notes. Organized and accessible.

Not locked behind a password.

Not scattered across three different devices.

*Accessible.* To the people who love you.

Phase two goes deeper.

A structured character model.

Built by you. In your own words. On your own terms.

So that someday — your kids can ask a question

and get an answer that actually sounds like *you.*

Not AI guessing.

*You* — deliberately, intentionally, on your own terms — leaving the answer behind.

---

## ADDRESSING THE CRITICS
*(Direct — look straight into camera)*

Now — I know what some of you are thinking.

*"This is creepy. This is playing God."*

I hear that.

But let me ask you something.

Is a photo album playing God?

Is a voicemail you saved because you can't bring yourself to delete it — is that playing God?

We're not replacing the person.

We're extending the conversation they never got to finish.

*"What if AI gets it wrong and misrepresents someone?"*

That's exactly why the framework matters.

The person builds it themselves. While they're alive. With full control.

It's not AI guessing who your grandfather was.

It's AI serving what your grandfather *deliberately left behind.*

*"This is only for tech people. Only for rich people."*

No.

The person who gets two sticks and a cross for a grave marker

deserves this as much as anyone.

That's why it's open source. That's why it's free.

That's why it runs on basic hardware — not a server farm.

*"What about privacy? Who owns this after you're gone?"*

You do. Your family does.

Local first. No cloud required if you don't want it.

No company mining your soul archive for advertising data.

*"Could this be exploited? Could bad actors use this to manipulate grieving families?"*

Yes. That's a real concern. And it's one this community will build protections against together.

No framework is perfect on day one.

But the alternative —

silence, a tombstone, a password nobody knows —

that's not acceptable either.

---

## THE PERSONAL
*(This is where you can get emotional. Let it happen.)*

I'll be honest with you about why I'm building this.

I'm 47 years old.

I've had a hard time my whole life expressing how I feel.

The way my mind works — logical, concise, non-emotional on the surface —

it's made it hard to perform feelings the way people expect.

I think a lot of that is just... how I'm wired.

And I have kids.

Kids who may have experienced my absence more than my presence

during the years when survival had to come before everything else.

And I want them to know —

every gap. Every absence. Every hard stretch.

You were on my mind every single day.

That drove me through things that should have broken me.

But I never found the right moment to say it the way it deserved to be said.

*That* is the problem I'm solving.

Not just for me.

For every father suffering in silence.

For every person who feels things deeply but can't transmit them in real time.

For every human being who deserves to be fully understood —

even if it takes longer than a lifetime to get there.

---

## THE CALL
*(Energy shift — invitation, not sales pitch)*

This is not a product launch.

This is the beginning of a movement.

I'm building this in public.

Every step. Every failure. Every breakthrough.

And I need people who feel this —

who've lost someone and wished they had more of them —

to be part of building it.

Not as customers.

As contributors.

This framework belongs to all of us.

Because this problem belongs to all of us.

---

## THE CLOSING QUESTION
*(Slow down. Almost a whisper. Let it land.)*

I want to leave you with one question.

Don't answer it to me.

Answer it to yourself.

...

*If someone you loved died tomorrow —*

*what would you wish they had left you?*

...

Put it in the comments.

Because that answer —

*that* is what we're building.

I'm Tydro Elite.

And this is Family Relic.

---
*[END]*
